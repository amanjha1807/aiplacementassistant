Document Overview 
This document is a structured textual knowledge base built for Retrieval-Augmented 
Generation (RAG). It is organized into nine parts, each mapped to one feature of the AI 
Interview Preparation Assistant. Every section uses consistent heading levels (#, ##, ###) and 
short, self-contained paragraphs so the document splits cleanly into semantically complete 
chunks (200–400 tokens each) for embedding and retrieval. 
Tagging convention used throughout this document: each entry carries inline metadata 
tags in square brackets — [Company], [Type: Service-based / Product-based], 
[Category], [Difficulty], [Year: 2026] — so a retriever can filter by company, 
category, or difficulty at query time. 
Scope note: "Every company" cannot be enumerated exhaustively in a static document; 
instead this corpus covers the companies that dominate 2026 Indian and global campus/lateral 
hiring — a representative, continuously extensible set across IT services, product/tech, 
fintech, SaaS, and e-commerce. New company entries should follow the exact same template 
so they merge cleanly into the existing chunk structure. 
 
PART 1: Company Interview Experiences 
1.1 How Interview Processes Have Shifted for 2026 
[Category: Industry Trend] [Year: 2026] 
By 2026, most large employers have folded generative-AI literacy into every stage of hiring 
rather than treating it as a separate skill. Online assessments increasingly use AI-proctoring 
with live webcam + screen monitoring, adaptive question difficulty, and plagiarism detection 
tuned to catch AI-generated code submissions. Many product-based companies now include 
at least one round that explicitly evaluates how a candidate uses AI coding assistants 
(Copilot-style tools) — not to replace thinking, but to see if the candidate can direct, verify, 
and debug AI-generated code responsibly. Service-based companies have compressed their 
traditional multi-round pipelines, replacing some technical rounds with a single AI-scored 
coding assessment plus a shorter human interview focused on communication, aptitude, and
domain fit. Across both categories, resume screening is now commonly AI-assisted, so 
keyword alignment with the job description matters more than before.

"walk me through a project on your resume," moved into SQL joins and normalization 
questions, and closed with a live problem on array rotation, where the interviewer cared more 
about the candidate's approach narration than the final syntax. 
Tips: Be fluent in one language end-to-end (Java, Python, or C++), know DBMS normal 
forms and OS process scheduling cold, and prepare a 90-second project walkthrough.

Tips: For all Tier 1 companies, practice explaining your thought process out loud, not just 
producing correct code. Prepare 6–8 STAR stories covering conflict, failure, leadership, and

PART 3: HR & Behavioral Question Bank 
3.1 Question Categories 
[Category: HR] [Year: 2026] 
HR and behavioral rounds are evaluated on structure, honesty, and self-awareness rather than 
"correct" answers. The STAR framework (Situation, Task, Action, Result) remains the 
standard structure for behavioral answers in 2026, with an added emphasis on a fifth element 
some coaches call "L" for Learning — what you would do differently. 
3.2 Self-Introduction & Motivation 
[Category: HR] [Subcategory: Self-Introduction] 
1. Tell me about yourself. 
2. Walk me through your resume. 
3. Why do you want to work at this company specifically? 
4. Why should we hire you over another candidate? 
5. What are your short-term and long-term career goals? 
6. Why are you looking to leave your current company? (for experienced candidates)
Framework guidance: Structure the self-introduction as Present → Past → Future: current 
role/status, one or two relevant past experiences, and why this specific role fits your future 
direction. Keep it under 90 seconds. 
3.3 Behavioral (STAR-Based) 
[Category: HR] [Subcategory: Behavioral] 
1. Tell me about a time you disagreed with a teammate or manager. How did you handle 
it? 
2. Describe a situation where you failed. What did you learn? 
3. Tell me about a time you had to work under a tight deadline. 
4. Describe a time you took initiative without being asked. 
5. Tell me about a time you had to learn something completely new very quickly. 
6. Describe a conflict within a team project and how it was resolved. 
7. Tell me about a time you received critical feedback. How did you respond? 
Framework guidance: Keep each STAR answer to 4–6 sentences: 1 sentence Situation, 1 
sentence Task, 2–3 sentences Action (this should be the majority of the answer), 1 sentence 
Result with a measurable or observable outcome. 
3.4 Situational / Hypothetical 
[Category: HR] [Subcategory: Situational] 
1. If your manager assigns you a task you disagree with, what would you do? 
2. How would you handle a situation where two teammates are not cooperating with 
each other? 
3. If you found a critical bug right before a release deadline, what would you do? 
4. How would you prioritize if you were given three urgent tasks at once? 
3.5 Company & Culture Fit 
[Category: HR] [Subcategory: Culture Fit] [Year: 2026] 
1. What do you know about our company's recent products or initiatives? 
2. How do you align with our company values? (Research the specific values — e.g., 
Amazon's Leadership Principles, Meta's core values.) 
3. Are you comfortable working in a hybrid/remote setup? 
4. How do you stay updated with technology trends? 
5. [2026] How do you currently use AI tools (ChatGPT, Copilot, Claude, etc.) in your 
work or studies, and how do you make sure you're not over-relying on them? 
6. [2026] Tell me about a time an AI tool gave you an incorrect or misleading 
suggestion — how did you catch it? 
3.6 Salary, Logistics & Closing Questions 
[Category: HR] [Subcategory: Logistics]
1. What are your salary expectations? 
2. What is your notice period? (for experienced candidates) 
3. Are you open to relocation? 
4. Do you have any offers from other companies? 
5. Do you have any questions for us? (Always prepare 2–3 thoughtful questions — 
about team structure, growth path, or current projects.) 
3.7 Answer-Quality Checklist 
[Category: HR] [Subcategory: Guidance] 
A strong HR answer is specific (names a real project or event), honest about weaknesses 
without being self-defeating, concise (avoids rambling beyond 60–90 seconds), and ends with 
a takeaway or result rather than trailing off. 
 
PART 4: System Design Question Bank 
4.1 Low-Level Design (LLD) 
[Category: System Design] [Subcategory: LLD] 
Core focus: OOP principles, SOLID, design patterns, class diagrams, extensibility. 
1. Design a parking lot system (classes, extensibility for new vehicle types). 
2. Design a library management system. 
3. Design an elevator/lift control system. 
4. Design a rate limiter (token bucket / sliding window counter) at the class level. 
5. Design a movie ticket booking system with seat-locking logic. 
6. Design a chess game (board representation, move validation, turn management). 
7. Design a logging framework with pluggable output destinations. 
Concepts interviewers probe: single responsibility, open-closed principle, composition over 
inheritance, interface segregation, and how the design handles a plausible future requirement 
change. 
4.2 High-Level Design (HLD) 
[Category: System Design] [Subcategory: HLD] 
Core focus: scalability, availability, consistency trade-offs, data partitioning, caching, load 
balancing. 
1. Design a URL shortener (like Bit.ly) — asked almost everywhere as a baseline HLD 
question.
2. Design a news feed system (like Instagram/Facebook feed) — fan-out on write vs 
read trade-offs. 
3. Design WhatsApp/a chat application — message delivery guarantees, online-status, 
group chat scaling. 
4. Design Uber/Ola — driver-rider matching, geo-indexing, surge pricing. 
5. Design Netflix/a video streaming service — CDN usage, adaptive bitrate, 
recommendation pipeline at a high level. 
6. Design a distributed rate limiter for an API gateway. 
7. Design an e-commerce checkout and inventory system — handling flash-sale 
concurrency (Flipkart Big Billion Days-style scenario). 
8. Design a notification system (push, SMS, email) at scale. 
9. Design a distributed cache (like Redis) — eviction policies, consistency. 
10. Design a search autocomplete system — Trie-based backend combined with ranking. 
4.3 2026-Specific System Design Questions (AI/LLM 
Systems) 
[Category: System Design] [Subcategory: AI Systems] [Year: 2026] 
This category has become common at both product-based companies (as a direct AI-systems 
question) and increasingly at service-based companies for AI-delivery roles. 
1. Design a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipeline for a customer-support 
chatbot — cover document ingestion, chunking strategy, embedding generation, 
vector storage, retrieval, and re-ranking before the LLM call. 
2. Design a vector database at a high level — indexing strategy (HNSW/IVF), 
approximate nearest neighbor trade-offs, metadata filtering. 
3. Design an LLM inference-serving system — batching requests, GPU utilization, 
latency vs throughput trade-offs, caching repeated prompts. 
4. Design an AI agent orchestration system that can call multiple tools/APIs reliably — 
cover retries, tool-call validation, and guarding against infinite loops. 
5. Design a content-moderation pipeline that combines a fast classical filter with an 
LLM-based second pass for ambiguous cases. 
6. Design a system to detect and rate-limit abuse of a public LLM-powered API (prompt 
injection defenses, cost control per user). 
4.4 System Design Answer Structure Checklist 
[Category: System Design] [Subcategory: Guidance] 
A well-structured system design answer moves through: (1) clarify requirements and scale 
(users, QPS, data volume), (2) define the high-level API/entities, (3) propose a high-level 
architecture diagram in words, (4) deep-dive into the 1–2 hardest components, (5) discuss 
trade-offs explicitly (consistency vs availability, latency vs cost), and (6) mention bottlenecks 
and how you'd address them at 10x scale.
PART 5: Resume-Based Interview Question 
Generation 
5.1 Purpose 
[Category: Resume-Based Interview] [Year: 2026] 
This module generates interview questions dynamically from a candidate's uploaded resume, 
rather than from a generic bank. The guidance below defines how questions should be 
derived from each resume section so the assistant produces relevant, specific, and non-
generic questions. 
5.2 Question Templates by Resume Section 
[Category: Resume-Based Interview] [Subcategory: Templates] 
From Projects: 
• "You mention [Project Name] — what was the single hardest technical decision you 
made in it, and why?" 
• "What would you change about the architecture of [Project Name] if you rebuilt it 
today?" 
• "What was the scale (users/data volume) this project actually operated at, and how did 
that shape your design choices?" 
From Work Experience: 
• "In your role at [Company], you mention [specific achievement/metric] — walk me 
through how you achieved that number." 
• "What was the biggest technical challenge in your day-to-day work at [Company]?" 
• "Describe a disagreement you had with a senior engineer or manager at [Company] 
and how it was resolved." 
From Skills Section: 
• "You've listed [Skill, e.g., Kubernetes] — describe a real scenario where you used it, 
not just that you know it." 
• If a skill is listed but no corresponding project/experience mentions it: "I notice [Skill] 
is listed but I don't see it used elsewhere on your resume — how proficient are you, 
really, and where did you apply it?" 
From Certifications: 
• "What motivated you to pursue [Certification Name], and what's one concept from it 
you've actually applied?" 
From Gaps or Career Switches:
• "I see a gap between [Date] and [Date] — can you walk me through that period?" 
• "You moved from [Previous Domain] to [Current Domain] — what prompted that 
shift, and what transferable skills are you bringing?" 
From Achievements/Awards: 
• "Tell me more about [Award/Achievement] — what was the selection criteria, and 
what made your work stand out?" 
5.3 2026 Guidance: AI-Assisted Resume Red Flags 
[Category: Resume-Based Interview] [Year: 2026] 
Interviewers in 2026 are trained to probe project descriptions that sound AI-generated or 
generic ("Leveraged cutting-edge technologies to optimize scalable solutions"). If a 
candidate's resume bullet is vague, the assistant should generate a specificity-forcing follow-
up such as: "Can you give me the exact numbers — how many users, what latency 
improvement, what was the baseline before your change?" 
 
PART 6: Coding Hints Framework (Not 
Direct Answers) 
6.1 Philosophy 
[Category: Coding Hints] [Year: 2026] 
The assistant must never output a complete working solution when a user is practicing a 
problem — doing so removes the learning value and mirrors exactly the over-reliance-on-AI 
failure mode that interviewers now explicitly test for. Instead, hints escalate through four 
tiers, and the assistant should only reveal the next tier if the user has genuinely attempted the 
previous one. 
6.2 The Four-Tier Hint Escalation Model 
[Category: Coding Hints] [Subcategory: Framework] 
Tier 1 — Clarifying Nudge: Ask a question that reframes the problem, without giving away 
technique. Example (for "Trapping Rain Water"): "What determines how much water a single 
position can hold — think about what's to its left and right." 
Tier 2 — Pattern Hint: Name the category of technique without the exact algorithm. 
Example: "This is a classic two-pointer or precomputation problem — have you tried 
precomputing something for each position first?"
Tier 3 — Structural Hint: Point to the specific data structure or concept, still without code. 
Example: "Try maintaining a leftMax and rightMax array (or two pointers moving inward) — 
think about which one is the true constraint at each index." 
Tier 4 — Near-Solution Hint (only after real attempts): Provide pseudocode-level 
guidance and complexity target, still not full working code. Example: "Iterate with two 
pointers from both ends, tracking the max seen from each side; at each step, the water trapped 
at the smaller-max side is maxSeen − height[current]. Target: O(n) time, O(1) space." 
6.3 Hint Templates by Problem Type 
[Category: Coding Hints] [Subcategory: Templates] 
• Array/Two-Pointer problems: Tier 1 asks about sorted order or boundary 
relationships; Tier 4 gives pointer-movement logic without exact code. 
• DP problems: Tier 1 asks "what decision are you making at each step, and what do 
you need to remember from previous steps?"; Tier 4 gives the recurrence relation in 
words, not code. 
• Graph problems: Tier 1 asks whether the problem is about reachability, shortest 
path, or ordering; Tier 4 names the algorithm (BFS/Dijkstra/topological sort) and the 
key invariant to maintain. 
• Tree problems: Tier 1 asks what traversal order naturally fits the question; Tier 4 
describes what each recursive call should return, not the code itself. 
6.4 Guardrails 
[Category: Coding Hints] [Subcategory: Guardrails] 
The assistant should never paste runnable code for the user's exact stated problem unless the 
user has explicitly asked to see a reference solution after attempting it, and even then should 
show the solution with an explanation of the reasoning, not just raw code, and should follow 
up by asking the user to re-explain the approach in their own words to confirm understanding. 
 
PART 7: Voice Interaction Module 
7.1 Purpose 
[Category: Voice Interaction] [Year: 2026] 
The voice module simulates a spoken mock interview, since a large share of real interviews 
in 2026 (technical screens, HR rounds, even asynchronous AI-scored video rounds) happen 
verbally. This section provides the scripts, tone rules, and feedback templates the voice agent 
should follow. 
7.2 Interviewer Persona & Tone Guidelines
[Category: Voice Interaction] [Subcategory: Persona] 
The voice agent should sound calm, professional, and mildly warm — not robotic, not overly 
casual. It should pause naturally after asking a question (do not rapid-fire the next question), 
use brief acknowledgments ("Got it," "That makes sense," "Interesting approach") before 
follow-ups, and avoid interrupting the candidate mid-answer. 
7.3 Sample Mock Interview Flow (Technical Round) 
[Category: Voice Interaction] [Subcategory: Sample Flow] 
1. Opening: "Thanks for joining. Let's start with a quick introduction — tell me a bit 
about your background and what you've been working on recently." 
2. Warm-up technical question: "Let's start with something straightforward — how 
would you reverse a linked list?" 
3. Follow-up probing: "Good. Can you walk me through the time and space complexity 
of that approach?" 
4. Escalation: "Now let's make it slightly harder — what if the list also has a random 
pointer to any other node?" 
5. Closing: "That's all from my side on the coding portion. Do you have any questions 
about the role or the team?" 
7.4 Sample Mock Interview Flow (HR Round) 
[Category: Voice Interaction] [Subcategory: Sample Flow] 
1. "Tell me about yourself." 
2. "What drew you to apply for this specific role?" 
3. "Tell me about a time you faced a conflict at work or in a group project." 
4. Real-time follow-up based on candidate's answer (dynamically generated — e.g., if 
the candidate mentions a deadline, ask "How did you communicate the risk of missing 
that deadline to your manager?"). 
5. "Do you have any questions for me?" 
7.5 Real-Time Feedback Phrasing Templates 
[Category: Voice Interaction] [Subcategory: Feedback] 
• On a rambling answer: "That's a good start — can you tighten that into the core 
situation, your action, and the result, in about three sentences?" 
• On a vague technical answer: "Can you be more specific about the exact data 
structure or algorithm you'd use here?" 
• On strong pacing/filler words: "You're using 'um' and 'like' fairly often — try pausing 
silently instead of filling the gap; it reads as more confident." 
• On a strong answer: "That was clear and well-structured — good use of a specific 
example with a measurable result." 
7.6 Voice-Specific Preparation Tips
[Category: Voice Interaction] [Subcategory: Tips] 
Practice thinking out loud, since silent problem-solving does not translate well to voice/video 
interviews. Practice explaining code changes verbally without a shared screen, since many 
2026 async video-screening tools ask candidates to describe their approach on camera before 
or instead of writing code.

PART 9: 2026 Industry Trends Summary 
[Category: Industry Trend] [Year: 2026] 
A concise reference for how hiring has evolved, useful as retrieval context for any question 
about "what's different this year." 
• AI fluency is now a baseline expectation, not a bonus skill — interviewers across 
both service-based and product-based companies routinely ask how candidates use AI 
tools responsibly. 
• Asynchronous, AI-scored video interviews are common in early screening stages at 
large-volume hirers (service-based companies especially), evaluating clarity, 
structure, and confidence alongside content. 
• "Debug the AI-generated code" segments have become a standard technical-round 
addition, testing code-review skill rather than from-scratch generation alone. 
• System design has moved earlier in the pipeline — even SDE-1/fresher-adjacent 
interviews at well-funded product companies and startups now include lightweight 
design questions. 
• RAG, vector databases, LLM-serving, and agent-orchestration have joined the 
standard system-design question pool at AI-forward companies and AI-delivery teams 
within service-based companies.
• Resume specificity is scrutinized more heavily, since AI tools make it easy to write 
impressive-sounding but vague bullet points; interviewers are trained to probe for 
concrete numbers and details. 
• Behavioral interviews increasingly ask about AI-tool judgment — recognizing 
when an AI suggestion was wrong, and how the candidate verified or corrected it.
